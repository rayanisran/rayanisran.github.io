Interactive Audio-Tactile Prototyping

Interactive Audio-Tactile Prototyping

The increasing use of haptic systems necessitates user-friendly software for creating interactive audio-haptic experiences. If you are an educator teaching the Cold War to blind students, it may be difficult to convey a graph such as the one on the left. To provide a potentially immersive user experience, we present a prototyping tool to easily and rapidly create multimodal audio-haptic designs on a Dot Pad Braille display.

The tool was created to support three goals:

   1. Allow designers to control which tactile pins to render, to create a desired tactile image.

   2. Provide an easy way to deliver targeted audio events upon touch inputs.

   3. Automatically generate a predefined audio-haptic experience for selected types of graphics.

A rendering of the multi-line graph is shown on the left. Users can tap on points for audio readouts of values, or pan across the plots to listen to a reinforced spatial representation. We also focused on mappings to allow for simultaneous discrimination of multiple graphs.

Comparing Audio-Haptic Systems for Rendering Charts

Comparing Audio-Haptic Systems for Rendering Charts

A sonified chart. (best played using headphones with spatial audio capability)

In the past decade, there has been considerable research into the use of audio-tactile graphics to present information in charts to blind and low-vision (BLV) users, yet comparatively little into sonifications and force-feedback rendering techniques. As tactile displays are generally more expensive or require bespoke hardware, recent commercially available force-feedback devices such as the Haply 2diy could act as substitutes for BLV users if they are able to interpret graphical content to a similar level of accuracy.

We conducted a study to compare sonifications alone against sonifications combined with three distinct haptic conditions: two force-feedback methods, and a refreshable tactile approach. We analyze semi-structured interviews to understand user preferences among the modalities, and then discuss the implications of our results on future multimodal audio-haptic designs.

HandsUp: Integrating Real-World Gestures into Digital Meetings

HandsUp: Integrating Real-World Gestures into Digital Meetings (Human-Computer Interaction, Fall 2020) 



A quick testing demo: gestures are used to respond to questions intentionally asked specifically to use them.

Popular videoconferencing apps like Zoom make it difficult for listeners to unobtrusively respond to a presenter. Zoom has attempted to allow users to signify indicators such as Raise Hand, Thumbs Up, and Thumbs Down, but the interface for doing so is hard to find and it is easy to miss the responses. Also, the responses do not decay, so unless the host clears all responses or all users undo their response after some time, the responses display even after becoming irrelevant!

HandsUp is a tool aimed at improving interactiveness in online classrooms. It contains both a hardware and software component. The hardware component consists of a wearable glove with embedded motions sensors that can identify basic predefined gestures and relay them to the computer interface. The software system is a desktop application that overlays a user interface on top of a videoconferencing window. The hardware and software systems are integrated to provide a seamless product that can efficiently translate hand gestures into highly visible indicators during a remote learning session.

Evaluating Tactile Acuity on the Forearm

Evaluating Tactile Acuity on the Forearm

Perceptual acuity to vibrotactile stimuli is of fundamental importance to effective use of the haptic channel for communication. An understanding of the variation in this acuity across different regions of the body could be important to designers of wearable devices. We characterized the influence of both spatiotopic and proximodistal distance on vibrotactile frequency discrimination performance through two experiments involving stimuli delivered to the volar forearm. Although we did not find statistically significant results for either study, the trends are suggestive of frequency discrimination being best at greater spatiotopic distance and smaller interstimulus distance on the sites tested.

Towards More Effective Data Visualizations with Haptics

Towards More Effective Data Visualizations with Haptics

In one mode, the user can search a data point to quickly navigate to it in a dense bubble plot.

Cognitive overload in complex multi-dimensional data visualization can cause challenges for users to comprehend, navigate, and determine statistical measures. To address this problem, we tested the feasibility of integrating vibrotactile and force-feedback haptics with bubble charts to test several haptic rendering techniques for visualizing data. The preliminary results suggest that force-feedback was effective in quickly navigating to points of interest in the data, and vibrotactile feedback was effective in representing higher dimensions and locating statistical measures of data.

Coastal Ocean Data Buoy

Coastal Ocean Data Buoy (BE Capstone Project, Fall 2016)

We designed and constructed a data buoy for measuring coastal wave-action. The design is based on a simple robust structure and mass-market electronics.

A significant dual-use feature of this design is that the structure can also be used for wave-energy generation, due to the isolated central structure of the shell. The electronics can be mounted in an easily-modified, rack configuration that can be removed from the buoy. In a wave-energy generation setup, the electronics rack would be removed and the central cylinder would become part of a linear-motion type generator.

At the moment, the buoy is dedicated to wave data collection, with an onboard inertial measurement unit and a radio-link to send real-time data to an offshore receiving unit. Basic GPS data are also transmitted. The buoy endurance is limited by the onboard, rechargeable batteries and is currently set to a maximum of 1.5 hours. Basic shakedown testing and data-fidelity checks were performed.

5-Band Audio Equalizer

5-Band Audio Equalizer (Fall 2020)

For ECSE 512 (Digital Signal Processing), I designed a 5-band audio equalizer and presented the MATLAB code and GUI implementation.

The equalizer was designed with five cutoff frequencies: a low-pass filter, three band-pass filters, and a high-pass filter. Implementation of the algorithm was tested by applying gains of +5 dB and -5 dB to each frequency band while keeping others unchanged at zero gain. Graphs that compare the frequency spectrum of the signal before and after equalization were created. The implementation allows the user to choose either music presets or their own values for the gain of each band.

4 DoF Software-Controlled Robotic Arm

4 DoF Software-Controlled Robotic Arm

As our final project in undergraduate for ME 4802 (Robotics), Hunain Qazi and I constructed a 4 DoF robotic arm. We used SolidWorks for the mechanical design, and C#.NET for the programming. An easy to use, custom-developed language was created to sequentially send instructions to the servo motors through an Arduino mounted on the robot, and a PID controller was implemented for precise control.
